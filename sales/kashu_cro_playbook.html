The 1 of 1 Philosophy
You are not selling processing. You are selling a decision framework. Every merchant has a processor. They're not shopping. Your job is to reframe how they think about their own P&L so that NOT switching becomes the irrational choice.

The Three Laws

1. Never sound like a processor. Every other rep leads with "save money on processing." You lead with a reframe.

2. Never compete on features. Compete on the frame. The merchant who sees processing as a cost center will always shop you on price. The merchant who sees it as a revenue line is yours forever.

3. Every interaction should leave them thinking "I've never heard it put that way before." If you sound like everyone else, you get treated like everyone else.

The 1 of 1 Test

Before sending any outreach, writing any copy, or making any pitch, ask: "Could any other processor rep have said this exact thing?" If yes, rewrite it. If no, send it.

When a merchant says "we've always used Square" or "we're happy" -- don't argue features. Strip it down to fundamentals.

Kahneman

Loss Aversion

The pain of losing is psychologically 2x as powerful as the pleasure of gaining. People will fight harder to avoid a loss than to capture a gain.

How to use it: Frame your pitch around what they're LOSING by staying, not what they're GAINING by switching.

Every month you stay with your current processor, you're losing $2,500. That's not a fee you're paying -- that's revenue walking out the door. In 12 months, that's $30,000 you'll never get back. The question isn't whether to switch. The question is how many more months of losses you're comfortable with.

6 Principles of Persuasion

Klaff

Frame Control (Pitch Anything)

Whoever controls the frame controls the conversation. In most sales interactions, the buyer holds the frame ("convince me"). You need to flip it.

  • Prize Frame: You are the prize, not the seller. "I close at 70%. My calendar fills up fast. If the math works for you, let's move. If not, no hard feelings."
  • Time Frame: Create urgency without being pushy. "Underwriting takes 3-5 days. If you want to be processing by next week, we'd need to submit today."

SPIN Selling Questions (Mapped to Kashu)

SPIN is based on 35,000 analyzed sales calls. The four question types build urgency progressively.

S

Situation Questions (Gather facts)

"What processor are you using currently?" / "What's your approximate monthly card volume?" / "Are you on interchange-plus or flat rate?" / "How long have you been with them?"

P

Problem Questions (Uncover pain)

"Have you ever looked at your effective rate vs what you were quoted?" / "How responsive is your current processor when you have issues?" / "Have you ever felt like the fees crept up without explanation?"

I

Implication Questions (Amplify the cost)

"If you're overpaying by even 0.5%, what does that add up to over a year?" / "What could you do with an extra $25K-$40K annually?" / "How many hours a month does your team spend dealing with processor issues?"

N

Need-Payoff Questions (Let them sell themselves)

"If I could show you in writing that you'd save [X] per month, would that be worth a 15-minute conversation?" / "If you could turn processing from a cost into revenue, how would that change your planning for next quarter?"

Authority

Win Without Pitching (Blair Enns)

Stop pitching. Start diagnosing. "Prescription without diagnosis is malpractice."

  • Position as expert, not vendor. You're not competing for the deal. You're evaluating whether it's worth your time.
  • Diagnose before prescribing. Run the statement analysis first. Never pitch without data. The diagnosis IS the pitch.
  • Charge premium, never discount. Where you win, you don't cut price -- you charge more. Kashu's value is in the surcharge reframe, not in having the lowest rate.
  • Walk away power. The willingness to say "this might not be a fit" is the most attractive thing you can say in a sales conversation.
Team Building

High Agency Filter (George Mack)

Screen every MRP, partner, and hire against these markers.

  • Green flags: Asks specific questions on the first call. Follows up within 24 hours with names. Has a network they can articulate. Challenges your pitch. Has built something before.
  • Red flags: "How much can I make?" before understanding the product. Wants everything done for them. No network. Agrees with everything (low-agency compliance). "Needs to think about it" without asking specifics.
  • The golden question: "If I put you in a room with 10 business owners who are overpaying on processing, what would you say?" High-agency answer = specific, actionable. Low-agency answer = vague, asks what you'd recommend.

Discovery

Sandler Pain Funnel (Mapped to Kashu)

The Pain Funnel is a progressive sequence of questions that moves from surface-level to emotionally charged. The goal: get the merchant to articulate their own pain so clearly that the close becomes inevitable. You don't pitch. You dig.

1

Level 1: Broad Openers (Get them talking)

"What are you focused on as a business right now?" / "How are things going with your current payment setup?" / "What were you hoping to get out of this call?"

2

Level 2: Surface the Problem

"Can you walk me through what happens when you get your monthly processing statement?" / "Can you be more specific about those fees? Give me an example." / "Why do you think that's been happening?" / "How long has that been going on?"

3

Level 3: Impact and Emotion (Where deals are won)

"What's the impact of overpaying on processing to your bottom line?" "What does that look like in dollar terms over a year?" "What have you already tried to fix this? How did that work out?" "On a scale of 1-5, how effective is your current processor at actually solving your problems?" "What happens if we don't address this now -- what does next year look like?" "Have you given up trying to find something better?"

That last question is the kill shot. "Have you given up?" triggers a visceral response. Nobody wants to admit they've given up. It re-activates urgency in deals that were going flat.

Qualification

MEDDIC Deal Qualification (Mapped to Kashu)

Before you invest time closing any deal, run it through MEDDIC. This prevents you from wasting cycles on deals that were never going to close.

M -- Metrics

What's their monthly volume? What are they paying now? What's the quantifiable savings? "This merchant processes $80K/month at 3.2%. Kashu saves them $2,560/month. That's $30K/year. The metrics are clear."

E -- Economic Buyer

Who actually signs? Is it the person on the call or someone else? "Are you the one who makes the final call on switching processors, or is there someone else involved?" If they say "I need to check with my partner," you haven't found the economic buyer yet. Don't pitch until you do.

D -- Decision Criteria

What matters most to them? Price? Speed of onboarding? Support? Contract flexibility? "When you evaluate a processor, what are the three things that matter most to you?" Then make sure your pitch hits all three.

D -- Decision Process

How do they actually decide? Timeline? Steps? "Walk me through what happens between today and you actually switching. What needs to happen?" Map every step so nothing surprises you.

I -- Identify Pain

What's actually broken? Not "they're paying too much" but the specific, emotional pain. "My processor never picks up the phone." "I got hit with a rate increase and nobody told me." "I've been meaning to look at this for two years and never got around to it." THAT'S the pain you sell to.

C -- Champion

Who inside the organization is going to fight for this deal when you're not in the room? For small businesses, the merchant IS the champion. For PE deals and multi-location businesses, you need someone on the inside who's sold on Kashu and will push it through.

Closing

Straight Line System -- The Three Tens

Jordan Belfort's framework: every prospect needs to hit a 10/10 on three scales before they'll buy. Your job is to systematically raise each score.

10

Product Certainty

"Is Kashu actually going to save me money and work better than what I have?" Raise this with: statement analysis, written pricing guarantee, live comparison.

10

Trust in You

"Is this guy legit or just another sales rep?" Raise this with: your knowledge of their business, your 70% close rate, your CRO title, your directness.

10

Trust in Kashu

"Is this company going to be here in 6 months?" Raise this with: merchant count, processing volume, BNP partnership, compliance track record.

The Looping Technique: When a prospect objects ("I need to think about it"), don't defend. Loop back. Resell the product (raise certainty on ten #1), then resell yourself (ten #2), then resell the company (ten #3). Each loop raises the certainty bar. The close happens when all three hit 10.

Tonality (90% of Communication)

Only 10% of communication is words. The rest is tone and body language. Key tonal patterns for Kashu calls:

  • Certainty tone: When stating pricing and guarantees. Declarative. No uptalk. "Your effective rate drops to zero." (Period. Not a question.)
  • Reasonable man tone: When asking for the close. Calm, fair, like you're asking something completely reasonable. "All I need is a recent statement and I'll have your comparison back in 24 hours."
  • Scarcity whisper: When creating urgency. Slightly lower volume, like you're sharing something not everyone gets. "We're pushing 15 merchants through underwriting this week. If you want to be in that batch, we'd need to move today."
  • I care tone: When addressing concerns. Genuine, not performative. "Look, I get it. You've been burned before. That's exactly why I do it this way."
Leverage

80/20 -- Perry Marshall

20% of your activity produces 80% of your results. Within that 20%, there's another 20% that produces 80% of THAT. The game is finding the highest-leverage 4% and going all-in.

  • Rack the Shotgun: Send one signal. See who flinches. The merchants who respond to your first outreach are the 20%. The ones who respond AND ask specific questions are the 4%. Spend your time there.
  • $10/hr vs $10,000/hr tasks: Formatting emails = $10/hr. Closing a $120K/month merchant = $10,000/hr. Building a system that closes without you = $100,000/hr. Know which one you're doing at all times.
  • Disqualify fast: If they don't have budget, if there's no urgency, if the decision-maker isn't on the call -- move on. The fastest path to 2,000 merchants is spending zero time on merchants who were never going to close.
  • Applied to MRPs: 20% of your MRPs will produce 80% of your merchant referrals. Find them. Feed them. The other 80% get the automated sequence. Don't spend equal time on unequal partners.
Scale

10x Is Easier Than 2x

Dan Sullivan's core insight: going 2x makes you do MORE of the same. Going 10x forces you to do DIFFERENT. 2x is incremental. 10x is transformational. You can't 10x by making more calls. You 10x by changing what calls you make.

  • 2x thinking: "I need to make 23 calls a day to hit 2,000 merchants." (Impossible solo. Leads to burnout.)
  • 10x thinking: "I need to build a system where 200 MRPs each bring me 10 merchants, and an AI voice agent handles the first call." (Different game entirely.)
  • The 80% purge: To 10x, you must drop the 80% of activities that only produce 2x results. That means: stop closing every deal yourself. Stop doing admin. Stop having calls that should be emails. Protect your time for the 20% that actually moves the number.
  • Quality over quantity: One PE deal = 10-50 merchants in a single conversation. One high-agency MRP = 20 merchants over 12 months. One viral MRP testimonial = 10 new MRP applicants. Stop optimizing for volume. Optimize for leverage.
Disqualification

The 5-Minute Kill Switch

The best closers in B2B don't waste time on bad deals. Within the first 5 minutes of any call, you should know if this is worth pursuing. Run this checklist:

Kill if:

  • Monthly volume under $15K (not worth the onboarding effort at this stage)
  • Decision-maker not on the call and no path to get them
  • Currently in a long-term contract with no willingness to pay the exit fee
  • High-risk vertical that won't pass underwriting
  • They want to "shop around" with 5 other processors (price shopper, not a buyer)

Advance if:

  • Volume over $30K/month and they're on interchange-plus or flat rate above 2.8%
  • Decision-maker is on the call or can be brought in within 48 hours
  • They have a specific pain point (rate increase, bad support, contract frustration)
  • They're asking specific questions about how it works (buying signals)
  • They lean forward when you mention the surcharge reframe (physical buying signal)

Perry Marshall's rule: For every unqualified prospect you cut, you free up time for a qualified one. The math always works in your favor. Cut faster.

The Up-Front Contract (Sandler)

Set the rules of engagement before every call. This eliminates "I need to think about it" because you agreed on the outcome before the conversation started.

Open every call with: "Here's what I'd like to do. I'll take about 15 minutes to understand your current setup, show you how the math works for your specific business, and then we'll both decide if it makes sense to move forward. If it does, I'll tell you exactly what the next step is. If it doesn't, I'll tell you that too. Fair enough?"

This does three things: (1) sets a time boundary so they relax, (2) establishes mutual decision-making so they don't feel sold to, and (3) gives you permission to close because they already agreed to a decision at the end.

Daily Operating Principles
Read these every morning. These are the 10 rules that separate you from every other rep in the industry.
01

Free Up Memory

Every open loop that can be systematized should be. Save cognitive bandwidth for high-leverage decisions.

02

Play Positive-Sum Games

You make money, the merchant saves money, the MRP earns residuals. Everyone wins. Say it out loud.

03

Pitch the Cascade

Always show the second and third order effects. Never stop at first-order.

04

Lead with the Reframe

The product is the last thing you mention, not the first. Teach first. Sell last.

05

Use Inversion on Stalls

"What would have to be true for this to never close?" Then address that one thing.

06

Be Unpredictable

If your pitch sounds like it could come from any other rep, it's wrong. Rewrite it.

07

Screen for High Agency

In MRPs, merchants, partners. Low-agency people drain bandwidth. High-agency people multiply reach.

08

Iterate Fast

The key metric isn't hours, it's iterations. Run more experiments. Kill what doesn't work. Double what does.

09

Verify, Don't Trust

Your assumptions, pipeline data, MRP activity. Check the numbers. Always.

10

Every Interaction Teaches

You're showing someone something they didn't know about their own business. The sale is the natural conclusion.

The Positive-Sum Stack

Naval says wealth creation is positive-sum. Status is zero-sum. Every competitor fighting over "who has the lowest rate" is playing a zero-sum status game. You moved the entire conversation to a different playing field. When the merchant is comparing you to their current processor, they're not comparing rates -- they're comparing "I pay $2,500/month" vs "I pay $0/month and might make money." There's nothing to compare. You're in a different category.
